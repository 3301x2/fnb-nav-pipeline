What you got right

You're profiling an audience the right way: size → spend → demographics → segments → co-shopping. That's the textbook shape. You're joining demographics from customer data, attaching behavioural segments, looking at what else they buy. The thinking is solid. The execution has two issues worth fixing now so they don't become habits.

The big rethink: who's the audience?

Your script defines the audience as "people who shop at any of 16 internet brands" — VOX, Afrihost, MWEB, Telkom, RAIN, all of them. That's not a VOX audience. That's an "internet category" audience. If your goal is to help VOX pitch for ad spend, the audience is VOX's actual customers, not their competitors' customers.

The 16-brand list isn't useless — it's the competitive set for benchmarking. Two different things:

If your question is...Then the audience is...
"Who are VOX's customers? What do they look like? Who else should pitch them?"DESTINATION = 'VOX'
The 15 competitors are for comparing VOX against, not for inflating the audience size.
"What's the total addressable market in the internet category?"DESTINATION IN (...16 brands)
This is what your current script returns. Useful for sizing the category, not for a VOX-specific pitch.
"Who shops in the category but NOT at VOX?" (poaching targets)DESTINATION IN (...15 competitors) AND UNIQUE_ID NOT IN (VOX customers)
Why this matters in the meeting: if you go in saying "your audience is 347,697 customers, age 41, R33k income, 46% female" — that's the average internet customer in SA, not the VOX customer. The VOX team can already get that from a press release. What they actually want is "your customers are X, the competitive set's customers are Y, here's the gap, here's who to poach." Two queries, not one.

The clean version — using the marts

Same analysis, in 4 short queries that each scan only the rows they need. Total scan: probably under 100 MB. Yours: ~2+ GB.

AVOX market position vs the competitive set

Use mart_destination_benchmarks. No joins, no scans of customer-level data. One small table.

SELECT
    DESTINATION,
    customers,
    total_spend,
    avg_txn_value,
    spend_per_customer,
    market_share_pct,
    penetration_pct,
    spend_rank
FROM `fmn-sandbox.marts.mart_destination_benchmarks`
WHERE CATEGORY_TWO = 'Internet & Data'   -- check the exact spelling in the data
  AND DESTINATION IN (
    'VOX', 'AFRIHOST', '3 NET NINE NINE', 'ZA DOMAINS', 'AXXESS',
    'COOL IDEAS', 'MWEB', 'TELKOM', 'EXACTTA GROUP SRL', 'VUMATEL',
    'G CONNECT', 'ACCELERIT TECHNOLOGIES', 'RAIN (DATA)', 'IKEJA',
    'WEB AFRICA', 'SUPERSONIC'
  )
ORDER BY spend_rank;
Bytes processed: < 1 MB. This is the table I built for exactly this question.

BVOX customers' demographics

Use mart_demographic_summary. Pre-aggregated by category × demographic group.

SELECT
    gender_label,
    age_group,
    income_group,
    customers,
    total_spend,
    avg_spend_per_customer
FROM `fmn-sandbox.marts.mart_demographic_summary`
WHERE CATEGORY_TWO = 'Internet & Data'
ORDER BY customers DESC;
Heads up: mart_demographic_summary is at the CATEGORY level, not the destination level. So it tells you the demographics of "everyone in the Internet category", not "VOX customers specifically". For VOX-only demographics you need a small custom query — see C below.

CVOX-specific customer demographics

This is the one bit where you still need int_customer_category_spend — but only as a tiny filter to grab UNIQUE_IDs. Then join stg_customers.

WITH vox_customers AS (
    SELECT DISTINCT UNIQUE_ID
    FROM `fmn-sandbox.analytics.int_customer_category_spend`
    WHERE DESTINATION = 'VOX'     -- only VOX, not the whole category
)
SELECT
    c.age_group,
    c.income_group,
    c.gender_label,
    COUNT(*) AS customers,
    ROUND(AVG(c.age), 1) AS avg_age,
    ROUND(AVG(c.estimated_income), 0) AS avg_income
FROM vox_customers v
JOIN `fmn-sandbox.staging.stg_customers` c
    ON v.UNIQUE_ID = c.UNIQUE_ID
GROUP BY c.age_group, c.income_group, c.gender_label
ORDER BY customers DESC;
The table is clustered by CATEGORY_TWO + DESTINATION. When you filter WHERE DESTINATION = 'VOX', BigQuery only scans the VOX cluster — typically a few hundred MB out of 3.6B rows, not the whole thing. Clustering is the bit that makes filtering on the indexed columns cheap.

DVOX customers' behavioural segment mix

Use mart_client_segment_mix. I built this exactly for "what does the segment mix look like for a specific client".

SELECT
    segment_name,
    segment_customers,
    pct_of_client_customers,
    pct_of_client_spend,
    fnb_pct_of_customers,
    index_vs_fnb
FROM `fmn-sandbox.marts.mart_client_segment_mix`
WHERE DESTINATION = 'VOX'
ORDER BY pct_of_client_customers DESC;
The index_vs_fnb column is gold for your pitch: it tells you whether VOX over- or under-indexes on each segment vs the FNB-wide average. "Your Champions are 1.8× the FNB average" is a way better headline than "You have X champions".

ECo-shopping (where else do VOX customers spend)

This is the one query that genuinely needs to scan the raw table — because we want all categories, not just internet. But we limit the scan by picking the VOX audience first as a CTE.

WITH vox_customers AS (
    SELECT DISTINCT UNIQUE_ID
    FROM `fmn-sandbox.analytics.int_customer_category_spend`
    WHERE DESTINATION = 'VOX'
)
SELECT
    cs.CATEGORY_TWO,
    cs.DESTINATION,
    COUNT(DISTINCT cs.UNIQUE_ID) AS vox_shoppers,
    ROUND(SUM(cs.dest_spend), 0) AS vox_spend
FROM vox_customers v
JOIN `fmn-sandbox.analytics.int_customer_category_spend` cs
    ON v.UNIQUE_ID = cs.UNIQUE_ID
WHERE cs.DESTINATION != 'VOX'             -- exclude VOX itself (we know they shop there)
  AND cs.CATEGORY_TWO != 'Internet & Data'  -- exclude all internet brands
GROUP BY cs.CATEGORY_TWO, cs.DESTINATION
ORDER BY vox_spend DESC
LIMIT 50;
Notice the two-line exclusion: DESTINATION != 'VOX' excludes VOX itself, and CATEGORY_TWO != 'Internet & Data' excludes the whole competitive set in one shot — no giant NOT IN list. This is cleaner than naming all 16 brands.

Patterns to remember

1. Check the marts first. Before writing SQL against int_* or stg_*, look at what's in marts.*. If a mart already answers your question, use it. The raw tables are 3.6B rows; the marts are kilobytes-to-MB.
2. Know the difference between "client" and "competitive set".
  • The client is your audience: WHERE DESTINATION = 'VOX'
  • The competitive set is what you benchmark against, not what you add to the audience
  • Don't bundle them together unless you genuinely want "the whole category" (which is a different question)
3. Clustering > filtering > scanning. When a table is clustered by a column (like DESTINATION on int_customer_category_spend), a WHERE on that column only reads the relevant clusters. So WHERE DESTINATION = 'VOX' is cheap; WHERE estimated_income > 50000 on its own is expensive (not clustered on income). Filter on clustered columns first.
4. Name your audiences. If your script profiles multiple slices, give each one a name and a comment. Never let a reader (including future-you) guess "wait, which audience is this section about?"
5. Look at "bytes processed" before you run. BigQuery shows it next to the Run button. If you're at hundreds of MB or more for a single audience profile, you're scanning something you didn't need to.

Summary

Three things to take away:

1. VOX = the client. The competitive set isn't part of the audience — it's what you compare against. Don't union them into the audience definition.

2. Use the marts. mart_destination_benchmarks, mart_demographic_summary, mart_client_segment_mix answer 80% of pitch questions without ever touching the 3.6B-row table. That's why they exist.

3. When you do need the raw table, scope the scan with a CTE first (pick the customer set, then join). Don't scan-and-filter — filter-then-scan.

Your shape is right, your instinct to attach demographics + segments is right. The fixes are just about which tables to reach for and how to define the question. Hit me back if anything's unclear.
